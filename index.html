About

I am a Research Assistant in the Lotter Lab at Dana-Farber Cancer Institute / Harvard Medical School, where I study how self-supervised vision models acquire and organize hierarchical structure in their latent spaces. My work focuses on the causal and geometric properties of representations and how inductive biases shape interpretable features that can generalize to rare or data-scarce biomedical domains.

I am currently completing my MBA at the MIT Sloan School of Management (expected 2026), where I have cross-registered in EECS graduate coursework including Deep Learning, Machine Learning for Healthcare, and Modeling with Machine Learning.

I previously earned a BA in Computer Science with a thesis in motion planning at Dartmouth College, and spent seven years at Google as a Staff Software Engineer and Technical Lead.

Research

My current work includes:

  • Investigating how transformer architectures spontaneously learn biological structure across depth, using disease progression as a testbed for hierarchical representation learning. See initial results: Blog
  • Adapting diffusion pseudotime techniques from single-cell genomics to infer disease trajectories from pathology embeddings across large foundation models (CONCH, MUSK, GigaPath, UNI2, DINOv2).
  • Designing computational frameworks that treat curiosity and humility as primitives for clinical decision support, enabling principled uncertainty quantification in diagnostic settings.
